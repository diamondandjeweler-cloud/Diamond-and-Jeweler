# Diamond & Jeweler — Senior Engineering Audit (2026-06-27)

_Successor to the 2026-06-17 AUDIT.md. Re-audited against the CURRENT, heavily-hardened codebase via a 7-dimension senior-persona pass (architecture, performance, clean-arch, frontend-UI, security, devops, debugging) with adversarial verification. Citations are file:line._

## Verdict — Overall grade: **B**

Diamond & Jeweler is a B-grade, genuinely launch-ready pilot whose architecture, security primitives, and front-end craft are well above what a solo-operated pre-launch product usually shows — held to a B (not higher) by ONE confirmed live security regression and TWO scaling cliffs that are isolated, not architectural. I verified the three load-bearing claims against the current tree and all three are real: (1) migration 0119 (`grant select … on all tables in schema public to authenticated`) is a table-level grant that clobbers the column-level revokes in 0091/0092/0105 and 0104 — IC-document PII (talents.ic_path) and proprietary BaZi internals (matches.internal_reasoning) are re-exposed to any legitimately-matched HM right now, and nothing after 0119 re-locks them. This is the THIRD recurrence of a documented leak and the RLS deny-suite gives false confidence because it only tests row hiding, never column isolation. (2) The match scoring path fires ~6-9 RPC round-trips per candidate across up to 500 candidates — thousands of edge→Postgres round-trips per single match generation — most against tiny-finite-domain IMMUTABLE/STABLE functions that should be memoized but are re-fetched per row. (3) 22 duplicate-numbered migrations (179 files, prod tracking ~0121 vs repo 0155) sit on a manual, untracked deploy path CI cannot see, so fresh-apply ordering is a genuine hazard and prod-vs-repo drift is invisible. The strengths are real and earned: a single shared scorer reused by 6 edge functions, fail-closed constant-time service-role auth, idempotent-by-construction payment paths, a poison-pill-protected match queue with cron heartbeat + dead-man monitoring, an excellent CI gate, and a hardened React 18 front-end with a real design system, working i18n at 1254-key parity, and an auto-recovering ErrorBoundary. The debt is concentrated in three god-files and an absent data-access layer (149 direct Supabase calls bypassing the 4-function lib/api.ts seam) — that is a maintainability tax, not broken layering. Net: ship-ready the moment the column leak is plugged; the matching engine is the one thing that will not survive real concurrency without an N+1 collapse.

**Scorecard:** STRONG NOW (lead with these): Architecture B+ (shared scorer, RLS-as-authz, async-decoupled LLM, cron-scheduled batch path — all verified), Front-end/UI B+ (real design system, skeleton/empty/error states, i18n live, axe suite exists), Debugging B+ (refresh-token race fixed, idempotent payments, poison-pill queue). MIDDLE: Security B (criticals pass is genuinely strong — Billplz HMAC, admin-escalation clamp, _shared/auth fail-closed — but ONE critical column-leak regression caps it), DevOps B (excellent CI gate, but split-brained deploy + advisory-only RLS suite + stale runbooks). WEAKEST: Performance B- (front-end ~A-, matching engine ~D — the single drag on the whole product), Clean-Arch C+ (absent data layer + three 1300-1600 LOC god-components + 1178-line matchForRole). Read it as: the PERIMETER is hard (auth, payments, RLS rows, CI), the CORE COMPUTE PATH (matching) and CORE MAINTAINABILITY (god-files, no data layer) are the soft centre. The one outright defect — not weakness — is the 0119 column re-exposure.

### Risk call (before scaling)

Three things matter before you scale, in blunt order. ONE — the 0119 column leak is the only item that should change your ship plan: it is live PII (national-ID document paths) plus proprietary BaZi IP exposed to every matched HM, it is the third recurrence, and your authz test suite structurally cannot catch it (row-only, advisory-only). Treat it as a P0 incident, not a backlog item. Until the column-isolation test is a HARD gate, assume a fourth recurrence is a matter of when. TWO — the matching engine is a correctness-clean time bomb: it works perfectly at pilot volume and will fall over at concurrency because every match generation is thousands of edge→Postgres round-trips against memoizable pure functions. Do NOT scale the talent pool or open concurrent HM match generation until the N+1 is collapsed; the async-LLM decoupling and 500-row cap bought you headroom but not throughput. THREE — the deploy split-brain (auto front-end + manual untracked migrations/edge functions, prod ~34 migrations behind repo, advisory RLS gate) is the risk that will turn a routine change into an outage: CI proves fresh-apply works but is blind to what is actually running in prod. The honest tradeoff: you have spent your engineering budget hardening the perimeter (auth, payments, webhooks, RLS rows, CI gate) to a genuinely high standard, while the compute core and the deploy enforcement lagged. That was a defensible sequencing for a pre-launch pilot — but the moment real volume arrives, the perimeter holds and the centre is where you bleed. Fix the leak today, collapse the N+1 and unify the deploy path before you scale, and the B becomes a defensible A- inside one focused sprint.

## Dimension detail

### Architecture — B+

This is a genuinely scale-ready architecture that has materially improved since the prior AUDIT.md (which graded architecture "B"). The core decisions are sound and minimal: a single shared scorer (_shared/match-core.ts) reused by 6 edge functions with zero duplication; candidate filtering pushed into a SQL RPC (get_match_candidates, LIMIT 500, ordered by feedback_score) instead of table scans; RLS-as-the-authz-boundary with realtime delivery gated by per-table SELECT policies; fail-closed constant-time service-role auth (_shared/auth.ts); and a clean, flag-gated Restaurant OS seam (App.tsx:74-115) that leaks ZERO restaurant imports into recruitment code and segregates the role union (types/db.ts:18-23). Two of the prior audit's headline architectural defects are now FIXED in the current tree: the "dormant scalable batch path" (process-match-queue) now has a 1-minute pg_cron schedule plus heartbeat + dead-man monitoring (0151), and the inline LLM call no longer blocks match persistence — matches insert with application_summary=null first, then pitches UPDATE asynchronously (match-core.ts:1207-1245). What keeps this from an A is concentrated, isolated data-access debt that is real but not architectural: the hot scoring path still fires ~3 RPC round-trips per candidate (decrypt_dob + get_life_chart_bucket + get_year_luck_stage) across up to 500 candidates, and pulls 27 system_config weights as 27 separate queries; 22 duplicate-numbered migrations create a real fresh-apply ordering hazard; and the client session layer (useSession.ts, lib/supabase.ts) is extraordinarily battle-scarred with hand-rolled locks, retry ladders, and a raw-fetch workaround for supabase-js — pragmatic and working, but a fragility surface. None of these require re-architecture; the ceiling is high and mostly earned.

_Scalability:_ Design ceiling is high and now largely earned at runtime, not just on paper. The two prior architectural blockers are resolved: process-match-queue is scheduled (0151, 1-min pg_cron + heartbeat + dead-man check) so the async batch path is live, and the LLM pitch no longer blocks match insertion (match-core.ts:1207). Candidate selection is set-based and LIMIT-capped (get_match_candidates, 500) — it will NOT degrade with talent-pool growth, which is the single most important scale property for a matching marketplace. The remaining bottleneck is per-generation, not per-user: each generation costs ~1,500 RPC round-trips (3 per candidate x 500) plus 27 config queries, all bounded and concurrency-capped (SCORE_CHUNK=50), so a single generation is slow-ish but generations are independent and queue-drained — horizontally scalable by adding queue workers. To reach true millions-of-users throughput: (1) eliminate per-candidate decrypt_dob by precomputing life_chart_character at talent write-time; (2) batch the two life-chart RPCs and the 27 config reads into set-based calls; (3) move Restaurant OS to its own schema so its 33 tables never contend with recruitment RLS/realtime/migrations; (4) upgrade off the smallest single-region compute tier (a documented prior 521 SPOF) before public launch. All four are isolated, low-blast-radius changes — no re-architecture required.

### PERFORMANCE — B-

The frontend is in genuinely good shape: route-level code-splitting with React.lazy + Suspense, vendor manualChunks, an injectManifest service worker that cache-firsts hashed assets, role-aware chunk prefetch, dashboard KPI caching, and realtime subscriptions that are tenant-scoped server-side with correct removeChannel teardown. The DB layer has had a serious, well-documented optimization pass (GIN/trigram/BRIN/covering indexes, PARALLEL SAFE candidate RPC, autovacuum tuning, candidate hard-filtering pushed into SQL with a 500-row cap). Where it falls down hard is the matching engine: match-core.ts scores up to 500 candidates with a per-candidate fan-out of ~6-9 network RPC round-trips each (get_life_chart_bucket, get_year_luck_stage, decrypt_dob, compute_age_match_score, get_peak_age_score, compare_nn_concerns) PLUS a per-colleague team-fit loop PLUS up to two industry_synonyms queries per candidate — several of which call IMMUTABLE/STABLE pure functions whose tiny finite domain makes them trivially memoizable but are instead re-fetched per row. At full pool that is multiple thousands of edge→Postgres round-trips per single match generation, and match-expire compounds it by re-invoking match-generate over an HTTP fetch inside a serial per-role loop with several N+1 sub-queries per expired match. The React dashboards are large (1300-1600 LOC) with zero useMemo/React.memo, and loadPreviews issues one RPC per candidate. Nothing here is a correctness bug, but the match path will not scale to high concurrency without an N+1 collapse. Frontend ~A-, matching engine ~D — net B-.

_Scalability:_ The system scales cleanly on the read/UI side (CDN-cached hashed bundles + SW, lazy routes, tenant-scoped realtime, indexed/parallel-safe candidate filtering, KPI materialized views) — public traffic and dashboard browsing will hold up. The hard ceiling is match generation throughput. A single generation is O(candidates × per-candidate RPC round-trips) of edge→Postgres latency, and match-expire serializes whole regenerations behind an HTTP fetch loop. Under concurrent hiring load (many roles regenerating at once, e.g. after a cron expiry sweep), edge-function wall-clock and Postgres connection pressure will spike together and approach function-timeout territory long before CPU/storage limits bind. The two highest-leverage, lowest-risk moves are (1) memoize the IMMUTABLE/STABLE life-chart and year-luck RPCs in-process (collapses the largest share of round-trips with zero scoring change) and (2) move per-role regeneration off the synchronous fetch loop onto the existing match_queue worker so expiry and generation scale independently. Do those before any traffic-growth event; the structured per-row vector and DOB RPCs can be batched in a follow-up. Treat all match-core changes as scoring-sensitive: gate behind the existing test oracle and byte-verify outputs.

### CLEAN_ARCH — C+

The codebase has genuine architectural strengths — a clean, well-documented Restaurant-OS seam (App.tsx:75-91 + lib/restaurant/ as a properly layered sub-module with its own store/types/context/format), a thoughtful role-type seam (types/db.ts) decoupling recruitment from restaurant, page-level organization by feature folder (auth/dashboard/onboarding/legal/blog/restaurant), and a localStorage dashboard cache with PDPA-aware design rules. But the recruitment CORE has no data-access layer: a thin repository seam exists (lib/api.ts, 4 functions) yet is bypassed by 149 direct supabase.from/rpc calls spread across 59 files, with the same tables (e.g. `matches`) hand-queried with hand-written column lists in 8 different route files. Presentation, data-access, business constants, and pricing logic are fused inside three god-components — HMDashboard.tsx (1621 LOC, single component ~1000 lines, 34 useState), TalentOnboarding.tsx (1569 LOC, 47 useState), TalentDashboard.tsx (1353 LOC) — and the server-side match engine is a single 1178-line matchForRole() function wrapping a 545-line scoreTalent closure (match-core.ts). Domain types (RoleRow, InterviewRound, InterviewProposal, CompanyRow) are redefined inline in 2+ route files instead of a shared model, and client pricing constants (URGENT_COST=9, POINTS_PER_EXTRA=21 in HMDashboard) duplicate the server's system_config-driven authority and can silently drift. No competing fetch libraries (no react-query/swr) — but that means 67 raw useEffect+supabase fetch loops, each re-implementing loading/error/abort handling. Behavior is correct and the seams show intent; the debt is structural fan-out and god-file size, not broken layering.

_Scalability:_ The data-plane scales well: the match queue uses FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED with bounded concurrency and a retry cap, partial GIN/btree indexes back the pre-filters at 100k-talent scale, and payment/points mutations are atomic single-statement CAS — none of these degrade under load. Two scaling caveats sit in the debugging surface. (2) The cron fan-out functions (match-expire, and the per-recipient notify loops) issue N sequential awaited round-trips per affected row; at thousands of expiring matches per 6h window a single invocation can approach the edge-function wall-clock limit and silently truncate its later passes — batching the talent/role/HM lookups (or pushing them into set-based SQL/RPCs) would make these jobs scale-safe and remove the partial-run heartbeat ambiguity noted in the match-expire finding.
